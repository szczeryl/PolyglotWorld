Lesson 5: Prices

Similar to most other countries in Southeast Asia, it is possible to haggle in some places. It is common practice for shop owners to price their goods a little higher than what they intend to sell them for, as to a certain extent, they expect the customer to request for lower prices.

As such, this section aims to equip you with the skills to navigate through making your purchases there.

Lesson 3: Food

In Malay, when you want to ask for the price of something, you use the word "berapa", which basically means how much.

To refer to the item you are negotiating, you do not necessarily have to say their names. Instead, a simple this or that works. This is "ini" and that is "itu".




The word for altogether is "jumlah". If you were buying many items and wanted to ask for the price at the end, you would use the equivalent of altogether how much.




When ordering food, you will always be asked whether you would be having the food here or taking it away. If you are having the food here, you should say "makan" or "makan sini", which translates to eat and eat here respectively. On the other hand, if you wanted to takeaway the food, you would say "bungkus", which translates to "wrap".





Now, something really useful for foreigners. Malay food is often spicy and if you were terrible at managing spice like me, you would always tell them I don't want chilli or just a little chilli . The most common type of spice is called " sambal", which is a delicious paste made mostly from dried shrimp and chilli (yes, it is not vegan).

If you wanted to ask whether something was spicy, you would ask "Pedas ke?", which translates to Spicy, yes or no?. If it was spicy, they will tell you "Pedas", else it would be "tak pedas". If it was just a little spicy (the most common reply being honest), it would be "sikit saja", which translates to just a little only.

If you do not want chilli at all, add " tak nak sambal" to the end of the order. If you wanted just a little spice, add "sambal nak sikit. If you wanted a lot of spice, you could say "tambah sambal", which translates to add chilli.




Obviously, there are many ways to say one expression and there is a lot more to ordering food (especially drinks). With this, that's all for lesson 4. See you at lesson 5!
